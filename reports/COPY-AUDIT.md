# Copy audit — high-impact tweaks (advisory)

Advisory only. These are before -> after suggestions for the orchestrator to apply to the live pages. The through-line: **lead with the outcome, tighten the value prop, sharpen the CTA verb.** Voice stays plain, confident, lightly noir.

---

## 1. Home hero — H1 + lede
The current H1 ("Open a file on any company.") is strong on flavor but light on payoff; the lede front-loads mechanics ("gathers... and assembles it") before the benefit.

- **H1 before:** "Open a file on any company."
- **H1 after:** keep "Open a file on any company." (on-brand) but add an outcome-first eyebrow.
- **Eyebrow before:** "a complete file on any company —"
- **Eyebrow after:** "know any company in minutes —"
- **Lede before:** "Company Dossier gathers the public record on a business — its people, hiring, money, locations, tech and headlines — and assembles it into one comprehensive, sourced dossier you can read in minutes."
- **Lede after:** "Read any company in minutes. Company Dossier pulls the entire public record — people, hiring, money, locations, tech and headlines — into one sourced file, so you walk into every meeting already knowing the room."

Why: opens with the result ("Read any company in minutes / know the room"), keeps the sourced-file proof, ends on the user's win.

## 2. Home meta description
Current is feature-list heavy and buries the outcome behind the mechanism.

- **Before:** "Company Dossier compiles a complete, sourced profile of any business — people, hiring, money, locations, tech, news and risk — from public signals into one file. Free. Web app, VS Code extension, CLI, and AI apps."
- **After:** "Know any company in minutes. Company Dossier turns the public record — people, money, hiring, tech, news and risk — into one sourced file you can trust. Free. Web, VS Code, CLI and AI apps."

Why: leads with the outcome verb ("Know any company"), trims to fit SERP width, "sourced file you can trust" adds a credibility hook.

## 3. Primary CTA verbs (site-wide)
The CTAs are consistent but a touch passive ("See a sample file"). Sharpen the secondary verb; keep the primary.

- **Before (secondary):** "See a sample file"
- **After:** "Read a real dossier" (or "See a finished file")
- **Before (ctaFinal sub on home):** "pick a company. read the whole story in minutes."
- **After:** "name a company — get the whole story, sourced, in minutes."

Why: "Read a real dossier" promises substance over a peek; the sub leads with the user's single action ("name a company") then the payoff.

## 4. /what-is-a-company-dossier/ — lede
The definition lede is accurate but long; the outcome ("read the whole company in minutes") arrives last.

- **Before:** "A company dossier is a single, sourced file that gathers everything the public record knows about a business — its people, hiring, money, locations, tech, news and risk — and lays it out so you can read the whole company in minutes instead of weeks of open tabs."
- **After:** "A company dossier lets you read an entire business in minutes — not weeks of open tabs. It's a single, sourced file of everything the public record knows: people, hiring, money, locations, tech, news and risk, laid out in order."

Why: outcome moves to the first clause; the contrast ("not weeks of open tabs") lands earlier where it does the most work.

## 5. /compare/ — index intro
Strong and honest, but the "what it is" sentence trails after a list of what competitors are.

- **Before:** "There are a lot of company-research tools — paid contact databases, funding terminals, enrichment APIs and news trackers. Here's an honest map of where Company Dossier fits: a free, public-source, fully-sourced first-pass intelligence file. Not a contact database, not a substitute for paid datasets — a fast way to read any company's whole story."
- **After:** "Most company-research tools sell you one slice — contacts, funding, or enrichment — for a price. Company Dossier is the free first pass: a fully-sourced intelligence file that reads any company's whole story from the public record. Here's an honest map of where it fits, and where the paid tools still win."

Why: leads with the differentiator (free, whole-story vs. one paid slice), states the value prop in clause two, and the "where paid tools win" framing earns trust before the table.

## 6. /compare/<tool> — per-comparison lede
The opener asks the question instead of answering it.

- **Before:** "Looking for a {tool} alternative — or weighing {tool} vs Company Dossier? Here's a fair, accurate read on what each tool does best, so you can pick the right one. Company Dossier is free, built from public sources, and every claim is sourced."
- **After:** "Company Dossier is the free, fully-sourced {tool} alternative for a fast first pass on any company — no seat, no export limit. Below: a fair, side-by-side read on what each tool does best, so you pick the right one (sometimes that's {tool})."

Why: answers the search intent in sentence one (free, sourced, what it's for), keeps the keyword "{tool} alternative" up front for SEO, and the honest aside ("sometimes that's {tool}") builds credibility.

---

### Pattern to carry across other pages
- Open every hero/lede with the **outcome** ("know / read any company in minutes"), then the mechanism.
- Make CTA labels promise substance ("Read a real dossier") over actions ("See a sample").
- In meta descriptions, put the benefit + "free" in the first ~120 characters; trim feature lists.
- Keep "public sources only" and "every line sourced" as the trust anchors — they are the differentiators, not decoration.
