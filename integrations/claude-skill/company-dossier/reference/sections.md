# The nine sections

Reference for what belongs in each section of a Company Dossier. Every entry in
every section must carry a source and a confidence tag (High / Medium / Low).
Public sources only.

---

## 1. Overview & identity

The "what is this company" snapshot. Establish the subject unambiguously before
anything else.

Goes here:
- Legal name and operating/brand name(s); former names if renamed.
- Official domain(s) and primary website.
- One- to three-sentence description of what they do and who they sell to.
- Founded date; legal form (LLC, Inc., Ltd., GmbH…) and jurisdiction of
  registration; registration/company number where public.
- Industry / sector and a rough size band (headcount range, public/private).
- Status: active, acquired, merged, defunct.

Primary anchors: company About page + footer, business registries (SEC EDGAR,
Companies House, etc.), Wikipedia, Crunchbase.

---

## 2. People & org chart

Who runs it and how it is structured — professional capacity only.

Goes here:
- Founders and current executive leadership (CEO, CTO, CFO, etc.) with titles.
- Board members and notable advisors, if public.
- Key functional leads relevant to the user's purpose (e.g. Head of Sales for a
  sales-prep dossier).
- A rough org/reporting shape if it can be inferred from public structure.
- Recent executive departures or arrivals (cross-reference §7).

Do NOT include: home addresses, personal contact details, family, or any
non-professional personal data.

Sources: company leadership page, LinkedIn public profiles, press releases,
speaker bios.

---

## 3. Hiring radar

What their open roles reveal about direction and pressure.

Goes here:
- Count and breakdown of open roles by function and location.
- Notable or unusual roles (a first-ever Head of X signals a new initiative).
- Tech/tools named in postings (feeds §6).
- Geographic expansion signals (hiring in a new city/country).
- Volume trend if observable (hiring freeze vs. aggressive growth).

Sources: company careers page, LinkedIn Jobs, Indeed, public Greenhouse/Lever
boards. Date-stamp — postings churn fast.

---

## 4. Money trail

Financial picture from public signals.

Goes here:
- Funding rounds: amounts, dates, lead/participating investors, stage.
- Total raised and last-known valuation, if public.
- Revenue figures or bands (only from filings or credible reporting — flag
  estimates as Low/Medium).
- Public-company financials: latest filings, market cap, key figures.
- Profitability/burn signals, debt, grants — only if publicly reported.

Sources: Crunchbase, SEC EDGAR, Companies House, funding-announcement news,
investor press releases. Prefer primary filings; tag aggregator estimates lower.

---

## 5. Locations

Physical and registered footprint.

Goes here:
- Headquarters (city, country; full address only if publicly listed by the
  company).
- Other offices, facilities, plants, data centers, retail/store presence.
- Registered/legal address if different from operational HQ.
- Remote/distributed posture if stated.

Sources: website contact/offices page, registry filings, public maps listings.

---

## 6. Tech fingerprint

The technology they build on and ship with — from public signals only.

Goes here:
- Web stack: frameworks, CMS, analytics, tag managers, CDNs visible on the
  public site.
- Infrastructure/hosting and email provider from public DNS/MX records.
- Languages, frameworks, databases, and tools named in job postings or the
  engineering blog.
- Public GitHub org: notable repos, languages, activity.
- Products/APIs/SDKs they publish.

Sources: the public website, public DNS records, job postings, engineering blog,
GitHub, BuiltWith/Wappalyzer-style public detectors. Do not probe, scan, or
attempt intrusion — observe only what is publicly served.

---

## 7. News & timeline

A dated chronology of notable events.

Goes here:
- Founding, funding rounds, major product launches.
- Acquisitions (made or received), mergers, spinoffs.
- Leadership changes, layoffs, restructurings.
- Awards, major partnerships, market entries/exits.
- Incidents: breaches, recalls, outages, controversies (cross-reference §9).

Present newest-first or oldest-first consistently, each event dated and sourced.

Sources: news search, company press/blog, trade publications.

---

## 8. Relationship web

The company's network of external ties.

Goes here:
- Customers / notable clients (logos, case studies, testimonials).
- Partners, resellers, integrations, alliances.
- Investors (cross-reference §4) and acquirers/subsidiaries.
- Competitors and close substitutes.
- Suppliers/vendors if publicly known.

Sources: company customer/partner pages, case studies, partner directories,
investor announcements, market-aggregator "competitors" data. Tag inferred
relationships (e.g. "likely competitor") as Low/Medium.

---

## 9. Risk flags

Concerns a reader doing diligence would want surfaced. Be factual and sourced;
do not speculate or defame.

Goes here:
- Legal: lawsuits, regulatory actions, fines, investigations.
- Financial: layoffs, down rounds, distress signals, going-concern notes.
- Reputational: controversies, negative press, sustained review patterns.
- Operational: breaches, outages, recalls, key-person dependence, single points
  of failure.
- Compliance/sanctions exposure if publicly reported.

Each flag must cite its source and carry a confidence tag. Separate confirmed
facts ("settled an SEC charge in 2023") from signals ("elevated negative reviews
citing support delays"). If nothing material is found, say so.

Sources: news search, public court-record aggregators, regulator sites, review
platforms read critically.
